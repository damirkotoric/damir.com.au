BrokerEngine

Design revamp of a feature-packed web app resulting in a quantum leap forward for the mortgage brokering product.

BrokerEngine is an all-in-one suite for real estate mortgage brokers. To use a tech analogy, BrokerEngine does for mortgage brokers what Atlassian's JIRA does for IT startups — it makes business processes more organised, helps businesses scale and reduces headaches for those in charge of overseeing all the work.

I came aboard a popular, mature and complex product that grew over many years, was feature-packed, and in need of a holistic, systematic design overhaul to modernise and simplify the user experience, and set the product on course to become a game-changer in Australia's mortgage brokering industry.

Helping BrokerEngine get acquired by AFG

I worked behind the scenes to architect an entire refresh of the product design. My Figma prototype was demoed during high-stakes acquisition talks between BrokerEngine and Australian Finance Group, and about a month later, AFG acquired a majority stake in BrokerEngine.

One-man design orchestra

Over the course of 6 months, I conducted the UX orchestra while simultaneously playing each UI instrument, so to speak.

My work touched on everything from grand strategy UX architecture and leadership on the strategic level — recommending which design system and Figma UI kit to choose, implementing said recommendations, introducing new design principles and UX patterns, choreographing a new core UI structure and site navigation. All the way to fine-tuning the minute details of the app on the operational level — patching up alpha builds of new features, writing microcopy for error messages and even picking a more appropriate icon to better convey the meaning of a button.

Mortgage brokering ain't easy

Mortgage brokers are inundated by numbers and calculations. In order to process a single client, a "deal" needs to go through 15-stages before the house keys are handed over to the customer applying for the mortgage. Mortgage brokering can feel like a turbulent bureaucratic roller coaster. I was astounded by the sheer complexity of this industry.

The mortgage broker's lexicon:

  • Product scenarios
  • Funding positions
  • Financing and refinancing
  • Principle and revision loan terms
  • Fixed and variable interest rates
  • Discount rates
  • Yearly and monthly fees
  • Monthly repayments
  • Payment options
  • Credit cards
  • Extras, packages and offers
  • Expected approval dates
  • Document sign-off
  • And many, many more...

And that's just lightly scratching the surface of a single client deal! Multiply the above by hundreds, or thousands of deals that a mortgage brokering business has to juggle, and soon you're swimming in an ocean of numbers, statistics, and the spookiest sea monsters of all: looming deadlines.

My goal for BrokerEngine was to simplify, simplify, simplify. Not to dumb down, but to elegantly simplify. My design goal for BrokerEngine is to be the rescue vessel that will alleviate stress and empower mortgage brokers to navigate this Turbulent Matrix-like Data Ocean with elegance and ease. To empower users by bubbling important information to the surface of the ocean, and give them a sense of control over their work. Mastery over their work, perhaps.

Leveraging the masters

My clients on this project, Will and Craig are the seasoned sailors of the mortgage brokering seas. With decades of real estate experience between them, they set out to build BrokerEngine to solve the exact complexities they had to encounter time and time again in their own line of work.

I leveraged their expertise as subject matter experts, and they leveraged my expertise to give coherence and structure to their product design, and together we collaborated across time zones to chart an exciting new course for BrokerEngine.

Introducing consistent, systematic components

BrokerEngine had inconsistent usage of UI components, and entire patterns were often reinvented on different pages. For example, the Filter component was used in a handful of different parts of the app, and each instance was different to the other. This is a lose-lose setup because it hurts both the business and the user. It's expensive to design, more expensive to develop, and dissatisfying to the user who has to relearn the same component many times over.

One of my first tasks was to identify any components that were used inconsistently and to design a single universal UI component system that would be standardised across the app.

Hit the lights! Dark theme support

Admittedly, this is mainly an eye-catching feature, though, with an increasingly remote workforce and more and people working flexible hours, a dark theme can ease the retinas during an evening session on BrokerEngine. This is also a differentiating factor from all the other products on the market. And it just looks damn cool!

Ant Design vs Material Design

Ant Design was the design system of choice when I joined the project, and I was keen to try out this fledging new design system from the people behind Alibaba. Later though, I would advise moving to Material Design.

Ant Design

  • ✅ A comprehensive set of components
  • ✅ Left-to-right and right-to-left variations of components for internationalisation
  • ✅ A comprehensive, though unofficial Figma UI Kit
  • ❌ Mediocre icon set with poor readability of icons, especially on smaller sizes
  • ❌ Uninspiring type system
  • ❌ The unofficial UI Kit clearly isn't maintained by the company and lacks some attention to detail, nor does it offer dark theme variations of any components

Material Design

  • ✅ Simpler and aesthetically more pleasing UI components
  • ✅ Excellent icon set
  • ✅ Excellent Figma UI Kit with both light and dark theme variations. Though I wish those variations were actual Figma variants instead of the light and dark themes being in separate Figma files, it's still better than no dark theme support.
  • ❌ Not as comprehensive as Ant Design
  • ❌ Equally uninspiring type system
  • ❌ Needs some tweaking to remove the "Android look". Namely, the ribbon effect and all caps buttons.

Neither Ant Design nor Material Design, nor their Figma assets were the holy grail of UI design that I'm seeking. But! They both save vast amounts of time, and personally, I'd pick Material Design as a base for future projects, which I found more robust. I only used the core MD atomic components (Text fields, Tabs, Radio Buttons, Checkboxes etc.) and I rolled out my own bespoke type system which is inspired by the more flexible Tailwind UI type system.
